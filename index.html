Core Topic

AI Project Cycle Explained Fully

The AI Project Cycle is a step-by-step framework used to complete an AI project. The chapter revisits it using everyday planning and project-building logic.

Definition: The AI Project Cycle is the cyclical process followed to complete an AI project.

Memory trick: P-D-D-M-E-D = Problem Scoping, Data Acquisition, Data Exploration, Modelling, Evaluation, Deployment.

01

Problem Scoping

Define the problem clearly. Set the goal and identify the parameters affecting the problem.

  • Understand the aim and objective.
  • Clarify what needs to be solved.
  • Study factors related to the problem.
02

Data Acquisition

Collect relevant data from reliable and authentic sources.

  • Data becomes the base of the project.
  • Reliable data improves the solution.
  • Wrong data leads to wrong outcomes.
03

Data Exploration

Study the collected data and identify patterns using graphs, databases, charts, maps, or flow charts.

  • Makes the data easier to understand.
  • Helps you detect patterns and trends.
  • Supports model selection later.
04

Modelling

Choose a suitable model, test options, and build the algorithm around the most efficient model.

  • Research different models.
  • Compare outputs.
  • Select the most efficient model.
05

Evaluation

Test the model on new data and check how well it performs.

  • Shows model quality.
  • Helps improve mistakes.
  • Prevents weak solutions from being deployed.
06

Deployment

Put the final AI solution into the real world so it can create value for users and stakeholders.

  • Final stage of real use.
  • Should work smoothly in practice.
  • Delivers real impact and benefit.

The chapter uses a greeting-card example to show that we already plan tasks in steps in real life, and the AI Project Cycle does the same thing for AI project development.

Important Topic

The Three AI Domains

Machines become intelligent according to the training they receive and the kind of data fed to them.

Medium Weightage

Statistical Data

This domain deals with data systems and processes where the system collects large data, maintains datasets, and derives meaning from them to support decisions.

Chapter example

Price comparison websites compare a product across multiple vendors using lots of data.

Minor detail

The extracted information is used to support decision-making.

Medium Weightage

Computer Vision

Computer Vision enables machines to acquire, analyze, identify, and extract meaning from visual input such as photos, videos, and sensor-based images.

Chapter examples

Agricultural monitoring and surveillance systems.

Minor detail

Input can also come from thermal or infrared sensors, not just normal photos and videos.

Medium Weightage

Natural Language Processing

NLP is the branch of AI that helps computers interact with humans using natural language, both spoken and written.

Chapter examples

Email filters and machine translation such as Google Translate.

Minor detail

The final aim of NLP is to read, decipher, understand, and make sense of human language in a useful way.

Most Asked Area

Frameworks, Ethical Frameworks, and Why AI Needs Them

This is one of the most exam-relevant parts of the unit.

High Weightage

What is a Framework?

A framework is a set of steps that helps solve problems in an organized, structured, step-by-step manner.

It also creates consistency and acts like a common language for communication and collaboration.

High Weightage

What is an Ethical Framework?

An ethical framework helps us make choices that avoid unintended harm and stay aligned with values, morals, and fairness.

High Weightage

Why Do We Need Ethical Frameworks for AI?

AI is often used as a decision-making or decision-influencing tool. If AI is biased, it can create harmful outcomes. Ethical frameworks help ensure that AI makes morally acceptable recommendations and helps prevent unintended harm before it happens.

The handbook specifically points to bias in a hiring algorithm that worked unfairly against women applicants as an example of why ethics matters.

Factors that can influence decision-making

The chapter explains that our choices can be influenced knowingly or unknowingly by many factors. These become important because AI systems can also inherit similar bias through data and human design decisions.

Classification

Types of Ethical Frameworks

Learn this exactly: ethical frameworks for AI are grouped into sector-based and value-based frameworks.

High Weightage

1. Sector-Based Frameworks

These are designed for specific sectors or industries. In this chapter, the most important example is Bioethics, which is used in healthcare and life sciences.

Other domains where sector-based frameworks can apply include finance, education, transportation, agriculture, governance, and law enforcement.

In healthcare, such frameworks address concerns like patient privacy, data security, and ethical medical decision-making.

High Weightage

2. Value-Based Frameworks

These focus on core moral values and ethical principles. They help us judge the moral worth of actions and decisions.

Rights-Based

Protects human rights, dignity, freedom, and autonomy.

Utility-Based

Focuses on the greatest good for the greatest number while minimizing harm.

Virtue-Based

Focuses on character, intentions, honesty, compassion, and integrity.

Critical Topic

Bioethics and Its Principles

Bioethics is the sector-based ethical framework explored in detail in the chapter. It is used in healthcare and life sciences.

Definition: Bioethics deals with ethical issues related to health, medicine, and biological sciences.

Why important in AI: It helps ensure that healthcare AI follows ethical standards.

High Weightage

Respect for Autonomy

People should be aware of how decisions are made and should not be controlled unfairly by hidden systems.

High Weightage

Do Not Harm

AI should avoid causing harm. This idea is called non-maleficence.

High Weightage

Ensure Maximum Benefit

AI should not just avoid harm; it should actively create benefit. This is called beneficence.

High Weightage

Give Justice

Benefits and burdens must be distributed fairly across people regardless of their background.

Minor Detail

Maleficence

Maleficence means intentionally causing harm or wrongdoing.

Minor Detail

Non-Maleficence

Non-maleficence means avoiding harm and choosing the path of least harm if no perfect choice exists.

Application

Case Study: Hospital Risk Algorithm

This is the best part of the chapter for understanding how ethics changes real outcomes.

The Goal

A company built an AI algorithm to help hospitals identify high-risk patients so healthcare providers could allocate resources effectively.

The Problem

Patients from the Western region who were given the same risk score as others were actually more severely ill, so the AI system was not treating groups fairly.

Why It Happened

The model used healthcare expense data as a proxy for illness instead of using actual physical illness. That made the model reflect spending bias rather than true medical need.

What It Teaches

If training data is biased, the model can amplify unfairness and harm the people who need help the most.

How the four bioethics principles fix the case

Autonomy

Users and patients should know how the model works and what data it uses.

Do Not Harm

The model should not redirect care away from the people who actually need it more.

Maximum Benefit

The model should help all groups fairly, not only avoid visible harm.

Justice

The system must distribute healthcare benefits fairly across regions, races, and backgrounds.
