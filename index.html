Full Chapter Notes

Core Concepts with Details

This section covers the chapter in the same broad order as NCERT and the syllabus.

Quick Revision

1. Discovery of Electron, Proton, and Neutron

  • Electron was discovered through cathode ray discharge tube experiments.
  • Cathode rays are negatively charged and move from cathode to anode.
  • Canal rays led to the idea of positively charged particles.
  • Proton is the positively charged particle in the nucleus.
  • Neutron was discovered later as a neutral particle inside the nucleus.
  • Atomic number = number of protons.
  • Mass number = protons + neutrons.
  • Isotopes have same atomic number but different mass numbers.
  • Isobars have same mass number but different atomic numbers.

Medium Weightage

2. Thomson and Rutherford Models

  • Thomson suggested that atom is a positively charged sphere with electrons embedded in it.
  • It explained overall neutrality but not the alpha-scattering results.
  • Rutherford's gold foil experiment showed that most of the atom is empty space.
  • A tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus exists at the center.
  • Electrons revolve around the nucleus.
  • Limitation: according to classical physics, revolving electrons should lose energy and collapse into the nucleus.

High Weightage

3. Bohr's Model of Hydrogen Atom

  • Electrons revolve only in certain permitted circular orbits.
  • Each orbit has fixed energy and fixed angular momentum.
  • Electron does not radiate energy while staying in an allowed orbit.
  • Radiation is emitted or absorbed only when electron jumps between two energy levels.
  • Energy of nth orbit: En = -13.6 Z^2 / n^2 eV.
  • Radius of nth orbit for H-like species is proportional to n^2 / Z.
  • Explains line spectrum of hydrogen quite well.
  • Fails for multielectron species and fine details of spectra.

High Weightage

4. Dual Nature of Matter and Radiation

  • Light behaves both as a wave and as a particle.
  • Planck proposed quantized packets of energy called quanta.
  • Photon energy: E = h nu.
  • Photoelectric effect supports particle nature of light.
  • de Broglie proposed that matter also has wave nature.
  • de Broglie wavelength: lambda = h / mv.
  • Large objects have extremely tiny wavelengths, so their wave nature is not visible.

High Weightage

5. Heisenberg, Orbitals, and Quantum Numbers

  • Heisenberg uncertainty principle: exact position and exact momentum cannot be known simultaneously.
  • Orbit is replaced by the probability concept of orbital.
  • Orbital = region in space where probability of finding electron is high.
  • Principal quantum number (n): shell, size, and energy.
  • Azimuthal quantum number (l): subshell and shape.
  • Magnetic quantum number (m): orientation.
  • Spin quantum number (s): spin direction.
  • s orbitals are spherical; p are dumb-bell shaped; d have more complex shapes.
  • Radial nodes = n - l - 1, angular nodes = l.

High Weightage

6. Electronic Configuration and Stability

  • Aufbau principle: fill lower-energy orbitals first.
  • Pauli exclusion principle: no two electrons in an atom can have the same set of four quantum numbers.
  • Hund's rule: pairing starts only after singly filling degenerate orbitals.
  • Order depends on n + l rule.
  • Completely filled and half-filled subshells show extra stability.
  • That is why Cr = 3d5 4s1 and Cu = 3d10 4s1.

Core Formulas

  • E = h nu
  • c = lambda nu
  • lambda = h / mv
  • Delta x . Delta p >= h / 4pi
  • 1 / lambda = RH Z^2 (1/n1^2 - 1/n2^2)
  • En = -13.6 Z^2 / n^2 eV
  • r_n = 0.529 n^2 / Z Angstrom

Common Mistakes

  • Always convert nm to m before using SI formulas.
  • For hydrogen spectrum, use lower n as final orbit and higher n as initial orbit.
  • In uncertainty questions, momentum p = mv, so Delta p = m Delta v.
  • For radial nodes, do not forget the minus 1 in n - l - 1.
  • For Cu and Cr, remember the exceptions before writing configuration.

High Practice Zone

Numerical Questions with Detailed Solutions

This section is intentionally large because Structure of Atom usually becomes easy only after solving.

1. A radiation has frequency 4.62 × 10^14 s^-1. Find its wavelength and identify the region.

Formula: lambda = c / nu

lambda = (3.0 × 10^8 m s^-1) / (4.62 × 10^14 s^-1)

lambda = 6.49 × 10^-7 m = 649 nm

Answer: 649 nm, which lies in the visible region (red side).

2. Calculate the energy of a photon of wavelength 400 nm.

Formula: E = hc / lambda

E = (6.626 × 10^-34 J s)(3.0 × 10^8 m s^-1) / (400 × 10^-9 m)

E = 4.97 × 10^-19 J

If converted to eV: E = 4.97 × 10^-19 / 1.602 × 10^-19 ≈ 3.10 eV

Answer: 4.97 × 10^-19 J or about 3.10 eV.

3. Find the de Broglie wavelength of an electron moving with speed 2.20 × 10^6 m s^-1.

Formula: lambda = h / mv

lambda = 6.626 × 10^-34 / [(9.11 × 10^-31)(2.20 × 10^6)]

lambda ≈ 3.31 × 10^-10 m

Answer: 3.31 × 10^-10 m.

4. A cricket ball of mass 0.10 kg moves at 100 km h^-1. Find its de Broglie wavelength.

First convert speed: 100 km h^-1 = 27.78 m s^-1

lambda = h / mv = 6.626 × 10^-34 / [(0.10)(27.78)]

lambda ≈ 2.38 × 10^-34 m

Explanation: This wavelength is far too small to detect, which is why macroscopic bodies do not show obvious wave nature.

5. Calculate the energy of an electron in the third Bohr orbit of hydrogen.

Formula: En = -13.6 / n^2 eV for hydrogen

At n = 3, E3 = -13.6 / 9 = -1.51 eV

Answer: -1.51 eV.

6. Calculate the radius of the second Bohr orbit of Li2+.

Formula: r_n = 0.529 n^2 / Z Angstrom

For Li2+, Z = 3 and n = 2

r2 = 0.529 × 4 / 3 = 0.705 Angstrom

Answer: 0.705 Å.

7. Find the wave number and wavelength for the Balmer line corresponding to transition 4 → 2.

Formula: 1/lambda = RH (1/n1^2 - 1/n2^2)

For Balmer, n1 = 2 and n2 = 4

1/lambda = 109677 (1/4 - 1/16) = 109677 × 3/16

1/lambda ≈ 20564 cm^-1

lambda = 1 / 20564 cm = 4.861 × 10^-5 cm = 486.1 nm

Answer: wave number = 20564 cm^-1 and wavelength ≈ 486.1 nm.

8. The average atomic mass of chlorine is 35.5 u. If it contains Cl-35 and Cl-37 only, find their approximate percentage abundance.

Let fraction of Cl-35 = x, so Cl-37 = 1 - x

35x + 37(1 - x) = 35.5

35x + 37 - 37x = 35.5

-2x = -1.5

x = 0.75

Answer: Cl-35 ≈ 75% and Cl-37 ≈ 25%.

9. For a 4p orbital, calculate radial nodes and angular nodes.

For p orbital, l = 1 and n = 4

Radial nodes = n - l - 1 = 4 - 1 - 1 = 2

Angular nodes = l = 1

Answer: 2 radial nodes and 1 angular node.

10. Calculate the uncertainty in position of an electron if uncertainty in velocity is 1.0 × 10^6 m s^-1.

Formula: Delta x × m Delta v >= h / 4pi

Delta x >= h / (4pi m Delta v)

Delta x >= 6.626 × 10^-34 / [4pi (9.11 × 10^-31)(1.0 × 10^6)]

Delta x ≈ 5.79 × 10^-11 m

Answer: about 5.8 × 10^-11 m.

11. How many orbitals and maximum electrons are present in the N shell?

N shell means n = 4

Total orbitals in nth shell = n^2 = 4^2 = 16 orbitals

Maximum electrons = 2n^2 = 2 × 16 = 32

Answer: 16 orbitals and 32 electrons.

12. An atom has mass number 13 and 7 neutrons. Find its atomic number.

Mass number A = protons + neutrons

13 = Z + 7

Z = 6

Answer: atomic number = 6.

13. Arrange 300 nm, 3 nm, 30 Å, and 300 μm in increasing order of energy.

Energy is inversely proportional to wavelength.

Convert everything conceptually:

300 μm is the largest wavelength, then 300 nm, then 30 Å = 3 nm, and 3 nm is equal to 30 Å.

Increasing energy: 300 μm < 300 nm < 30 Å = 3 nm

14. Why is Cu written as 3d10 4s1 and not 3d9 4s2?

This is not a pure arithmetic numerical, but it is an exam favorite explanation.

Completely filled d-subshells are extra stable because of symmetrical distribution and exchange energy.

So one electron shifts from 4s to 3d and Cu becomes 3d10 4s1.

Answer: extra stability of completely filled 3d10 causes the exception.

Understanding Through Stories

Case Study Style Explanations

These are easier to remember than isolated theory lines.

Case Study 1: Cathode Ray Tube

Problem: What are cathode rays?

Observation: They move towards the positive plate and get deflected by electric and magnetic fields.

Conclusion: They are made of negatively charged particles, i.e. electrons.

Case Study 2: Gold Foil Experiment

Problem: Where is positive charge located in an atom?

Observation: Most alpha-particles passed straight; a few deflected sharply.

Conclusion: Most of atom is empty, and a tiny dense positively charged nucleus exists at the center.

Case Study 3: Hydrogen Spectrum

Problem: Why does hydrogen give discrete lines instead of continuous colors?

Observation: Only specific wavelengths appear.

Conclusion: Electron energy levels are quantized, and light is emitted only during fixed transitions.

Rapid Recall Sheet

Definition Level

  • Atomic number = number of protons.
  • Mass number = protons + neutrons.
  • Isotopes: same atomic number, different mass number.
  • Isobars: same mass number, different atomic number.

Quantum Level

  • n gives shell, size, and energy.
  • l gives subshell and shape.
  • m gives orientation.
  • s gives spin direction.
  • Radial nodes = n - l - 1, angular nodes = l.

Numerical Level

  • Always convert units carefully.
  • For Balmer use final orbit n = 2.
  • For wavelength-energy questions, use hc/lambda.
  • For uncertainty, use Delta p = m Delta v.
